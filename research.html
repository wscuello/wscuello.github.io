Research interests

In my research, I am interested in how environmental fluctuations play a role in demographic stochasticity. In particular, I investigate, by data-driven and theoretical means, how environmental fluctuations affect single- and multispecies systems. I am also interested in constructing or using mathematical frameworks for analyzing species-to-species interactions.

For my thesis, and under the supervision of my advisor, Dr. Sebastian Schreiber at UC Davis, I created a mathematical framework for analyzing whether an arbitrary number of interacting species would stochastically persist in a randomly fluctuating environment.

Currently, I am working under Dr. Konstantin Mischaikow at Rutgers University to adapt their mathematical framework for analyzing gene regulatory networks to ecological networks.

Most recent publication:

Predicting evolutionarily stable strategies from functional responses of Sonoran Desert annuals to precipitation. W.S. Cuello, J.R. Gremer, P.C. Trimmer, A. Sih, and S.J. Schreiber (2019). Proceedings of the Royal Society B, Biological Sciences 286: 20182613.

In this paper, we tracked the year-to-year densities of ten Sonoran Desert winter annuals by creating ten bet-hedging, stochastic models. We parameterized these models using 30 years of seed-yield and precipitation data. In doing so, fluctuations in our population models' outputs were driven solely by random fluctuations in precipitation. Here, we were able to predict average germination rates for each species by solving for evolutionarily stable strategies (ESS values). We also statistically found to what extent seed survivorship and functional responses to precipitation drove these average germination rates and ESS values.
